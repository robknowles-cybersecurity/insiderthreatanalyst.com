Human Risk
The 8 / 80 Rule
8% of employees are responsible for 80% of security incidents. This isn't a technology problem — it's a human behavior problem. And it cuts across every risk domain you already manage.
So here's the question: does human risk deserve its own domain in your enterprise risk taxonomy — alongside operational risk, credit risk, market risk? Or is it a cross-cutting factor that lives inside every other domain?

Mimecast, State of Human Risk 2026
3
Two Ways to See It
For a dedicated domain

Dedicated ownership. Dedicated budget. Dedicated metrics. Without a home, human risk is everyone's problem and nobody's program. You can't improve what you don't measure, and you can't measure what doesn't have a name.

Against a dedicated domain

Siloing creates the illusion that it's contained. Human risk is operational risk, is compliance risk, is third-party risk. A dedicated domain might let other risk owners abdicate: "that's the human risk team's problem."

I've argued both sides. I don't think there's a clean answer — and I'd genuinely like to hear how this group thinks about it. But hold that thought, because the picture just got more complicated.
4
The Shift
The Insider That Doesn't Attend Your Security Awareness Training
The traditional insider threat picture assumed a human with credentials, access, and either intent or negligence. As of 2026, the fastest-growing population of "insiders" in your organization isn't human. They don't have a badge. They don't take PTO. And your insider threat program almost certainly isn't watching them.
5
Shadow AI: Three Waves
1
Data leakage (2023–2024)
Employees paste sensitive data into ChatGPT. A familiar DLP problem in a new wrapper. Security teams responded with the controls they already had.
2
Autonomous agents (2025–now)
Employees deploy AI agents with API access that chain actions across services at machine speed. Agents inherit permissions, operate continuously, and make decisions without human review. 47% of enterprise AI use happens through personal accounts outside SSO.
3
Adversarial exploitation (emerging)
Threat actors exploit agent infrastructure to move laterally. The same autonomous capabilities that make agents productive make them high-value targets. 79% of organizations lack visibility into AI agents in their environment.
CSA, "Shadow AI Agents" (May 2026) · DTEX Insider Threat Report 2026 · CrowdStrike Global Threat Report 2026
6
The Governance Gap
19%
of organizations classify AI agents as equivalent to human insiders
Join the poll · menti.com · 9454 6868
Your BCP and incident response plans account for a rogue employee or a compromised credential. Consider whether they also account for an autonomous agent that inherited a senior analyst's permissions — operating at machine speed, 24/7, across every system it can reach, with no break and no second approval.
The blast radius math changes when the "insider" doesn't sleep and doesn't hesitate.
DTEX, Cost of Insider Risks 2026
7
Three Things to Consider This Quarter
1 · Inventory

Identify where AI agents are operating in your environment — sanctioned and unsanctioned. Consider tools like Microsoft's Security Copilot, which now discovers 18 agent types on endpoints. You can't govern what you can't see.

2 · Assign an owner

Decide who owns autonomous-agent risk before an incident does — insider risk, incident response, or identity management. The home varies by org; what can't vary is that agents with credentials, access, and autonomy get a named owner and a behavioral baseline, instead of falling through the gap between teams.

3 · Exercise

Consider adding an "autonomous agent incident" scenario to your next tabletop. What does your IRP look like when the insider is software you deployed on purpose?
