How Do I Get People to Care About My Program?

The hardest part of insider threat isn't detection engineering. It's getting leadership buy-in, sustaining workforce awareness, and making people understand why this matters before something goes wrong. This page is your toolkit.

National Insider Threat Awareness Month (NITAM)

Every September since 2019, DCSA, CDSE, NITTF, and NCSC run a national awareness campaign. It's the single best annual touchpoint for insider threat program visibility. 30,000+ visits to the NITAM website in 2025. If you're not leveraging NITAM, you're leaving free engagement on the table.

NITAM timeline
2019Inaugural Year
First NITAM, launched by NCSC and NITTF. Established September as the annual awareness month. Initial focus on building baseline understanding across the federal workforce.
2020Resilience
Focused on organizational and individual resilience during COVID-19. Remote work created new insider threat vectors — monitoring gaps, isolation, financial stress. The theme was prescient.
2021Protecting Critical Assets
Refocused on core mission: identifying, protecting, and monitoring access to the assets that matter most. Supply chain and critical infrastructure emphasis post-SolarWinds.
2022Countering Risk in Digital Spaces
The hybrid work reality. Digital collaboration tools, cloud storage, personal device use — all expanded the attack surface. Insider risk in Slack, Teams, SharePoint, and SaaS platforms.
2023Bystander Engagement
Shift from "see something, say something" to structured bystander intervention. Reporting culture, psychological safety, and removing stigma from raising concerns. The human sensor network.
2024Deter, Detect, Mitigate
Back to fundamentals — the three pillars of effective insider threat programs. Deterrence through policy and culture, detection through monitoring and analytics, mitigation through response and recovery.
2025Partnering for Progress
Cross-functional collaboration. Insider threat is not a security-only problem — it requires HR, legal, IT, management, and employee assistance. Building coalitions, not silos.
2026TBD
Theme typically announced in spring/summer. Watch CDSE NITAM page for updates.
How to use NITAM: Don't just forward the CDSE email. Build a month-long internal campaign: week 1 = leadership message, week 2 = tabletop exercise, week 3 = team training, week 4 = recognition. Use CDSE's free graphics, videos, and templates as your base. Customize with internal metrics and case references. NITAM is the scaffolding — you build the house.

Building an awareness campaign

Effective awareness isn't a once-a-year checkbox. It's a sustained effort to make insider threat part of organizational culture. Here's what works.

Leadership engagement
🎯
Speak their language
Executives don't care about detection rules. They care about risk to revenue, regulatory penalties, reputational damage, and loss of competitive advantage. Frame every briefing around business impact, not technical capability. "We detected 12 anomalies" means nothing. "An employee downloaded our entire client list the week before leaving for a competitor" does.
📊
Bring metrics that matter
Time-to-detect, cases-to-resolution, policy violation trends, near-miss counts, and benchmark comparisons. The DTEX Insider Risk Report and Proofpoint Threat Reports provide industry benchmarks you can reference.
📅
Quarterly, not annual
Annual briefings create annual attention. Quarterly touchpoints with leadership — even 15 minutes — keep the program visible and funded. Tie updates to business events: M&A activity, RIFs, reorganizations, compliance audits.
Workforce awareness
🧠
Make it about them, not surveillance
The fastest way to kill a program is to make employees feel watched. Frame awareness around protecting the organization and protecting them — from social engineering, from being scapegoated, from inadvertent violations. The program exists to help, not to hunt.
🎬
Use real stories, not slides
Case studies hit harder than bullet points. The Hanssen, Montes, and Snowden cases are public, well-documented, and fascinating. Code42's The Inside Man web series dramatizes insider scenarios — free, short episodes, good for team discussions. See our recommended media for more.
🎯
Test your analysts' biases
Insider threat analysis is high-stakes, low-base-rate work. Cognitive biases — confirmation bias, anchoring, availability heuristic — are the real adversary. Try the Spot the Bias quiz on the framework page. Use it in team training to spark discussion about analytical rigor.

Tabletop exercises

Nothing builds stakeholder understanding faster than a well-run tabletop. Put leadership and cross-functional partners in the decision seat for 90 minutes and watch the lightbulbs turn on.

Running a tabletop
📋
Scenario design
Base scenarios on real cases adapted to your organization. A data exfiltration by a departing employee, a compromised credential escalation, a trusted insider with gradual behavioral changes. CDSE provides scenario templates. Inject decision points: "HR wants to fire immediately, Legal wants to investigate further, the CISO wants to preserve evidence. What do you do?"
👥
Who to include
The whole point is cross-functional engagement. Security, HR, Legal, IT, Privacy, Compliance, and at least one business unit leader. If your tabletop only has security people, you're rehearsing, not exercising. Include an executive sponsor as observer.
📝
After-action
Document gaps, decision delays, and policy conflicts uncovered during the exercise. These become your program roadmap items. Share the after-action summary with leadership — it's the most persuasive artifact you'll produce all year.
CDSE games & exercises: CDSE offers free interactive games and tabletop exercise kits designed specifically for insider threat awareness. Available through the NITAM resource page and the CDSE training portal. Use them as starting points — customize with your own organizational context, policies, and reporting channels.

Recommended media

Books, podcasts, films, and video courses curated for insider threat practitioners. Use these for personal development, team training, or awareness campaigns.

Full media library: The complete curated list — 12+ books, 5 podcasts, 11 films/TV shows, and 6 video courses — lives on the framework page's Development tab. Below are the top picks for stakeholder engagement specifically.
Top picks for awareness & engagement
📖
Inside Jobs — Payne, Hanson, Wojtasiak
Best book to hand a non-security executive. Makes the business case for insider risk programs without jargon. Give this to your CISO, CFO, or GC.
🎬
The Inside Man — Code42 (web series)
Short dramatized insider threat scenarios. Free with signup. Perfect for team training sessions — watch an episode, discuss what you'd detect and what you'd miss.
🎬
Breach (2007) — Billy Ray
The Robert Hanssen case dramatized. Best insider threat film for showing how access, trust, and compartmentalization failures compound over decades. Good for leadership audiences.
📖
Psychology of Intelligence Analysis — Heuer (CIA, free PDF)
The foundational text on cognitive biases in analysis. Chapter 8 (Analysis of Competing Hypotheses) is the single most important chapter for insider threat analysts. Free from CIA.
🎙
Darknet Diaries — podcast
Narrative cybersecurity storytelling. Several episodes feature insider threats. Compelling enough to share with non-security colleagues. Good gateway content.
The engagement toolkit is the program.

Detection engineering finds the needle. Stakeholder engagement shrinks the haystack. The best insider threat programs invest equally in both. If you've built the technical capability but can't explain why it matters to a room of executives in five minutes — start here.
